Multi-Factor Health Risk Assessment for Newborns and Adoloscents

Data-Driven Insights for Infant Health Using Classification, Regression & Clustering

Introduction

Research Topic & Significance

Our research focuses on developing a composite health risk index for newborns and infants using population-level health data. The goal is to identify patterns and disparities in infant health outcomes, such as assisted ventilation, birth complications, and mortality, by leveraging different social economic factors. This research is important because infant health is a key indicator of overall population well-being and healthcare system effectiveness. The importance of this work is underscored by national statistics. For example, in 2022, non-Hispanic Black infants experienced an infant mortality rate of 10.9 per 1,000 live births, compared to just 3.5 per 1,000 among non-Hispanic Asian infants (CDC, 2023 - Figure 1). Similarly, geographic disparities persist: states like Mississippi and Arkansas report mortality rates above 8 per 1,000, while western states such as California and Oregon remain closer to 4 per 1,000 (Figure 2). These disparities highlight the urgent need for data-driven tools that not only describe these differences but also predict and classify risk so that interventions can be targeted where they are most needed. By combining clustering, regression, and classification methods, our research seeks to move beyond descriptive statistics into predictive modeling of risk profiles. This approach allows us to uncover hidden risk groups, quantify how different social and demographic factors contribute to poor outcomes, and create actionable categories such as low, medium, and high-risk populations. In doing so, our project addresses a pressing social issue: reducing health inequities and improving survival outcomes for infants across diverse communities in the United States.

Visual Source: CDC National Center for Health Statistics ‚Äì Infant Mortality by Race and State, 2022

Stakeholders

Infant mortality affects many people and groups, with impacts that go beyond just health numbers. Families and communities experience deep emotional and psychological stress when a baby is born too early or dies. Medical institutions and professionals face more pressure to care for mothers and babies who are at risk. The healthcare system as a whole is affected through higher medical costs and increased demand for special services, like neonatal intensive care units (NICUs). Hospitals also face extra pressure to address health gaps among different populations. Governments and policymakers are impacted as well, because high infant death rates reveal problems in access to healthcare, living conditions, and support programs for mothers. These issues require attention, planning, and allocation of resources to improve outcomes. Employers and the economy feel indirect effects too, since poor maternal and infant health can reduce workforce participation and create financial strain for families. On a larger scale, infant mortality highlights inequalities across different social groups. This shows that infant mortality is not just a medical issue, but also a social and economic problem with effects that reach far across society.

Existing Solutions & Gaps

Current solutions in maternal and fetal health increasingly rely on machine learning (ML) methods to predict adverse outcomes. The study by Cho et al. [1] used six ML models on a national cohort of very low birth weight infants to predict risks like extremely preterm birth, extremely low birth weight, and small-for-gestational-age. Among these, the random forest model performed best (accuracy 0.79, AUC 0.72) and identified maternal age, birth month, PM10 air pollution, and pregnancy complications as key predictors. Similarly, Mennickent et al. [2] reviewed a wide body of ML applications in maternal-fetal health, covering gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, perinatal death, and stillbirth prediction. They noted that non-linear ML methods such as random forest, gradient boosting, and deep neural networks are most commonly applied, achieving high predictive performance across conditions. Although machine learning has shown strong promise in predicting adverse maternal and fetal outcomes, most existing studies rely heavily on classification models. These methods primarily assign risk categories (e.g., high vs. low risk, diseased vs. healthy) but do not fully capture the continuous nature of clinical parameters where regression could provide richer insights. Likewise, clustering approaches that might reveal hidden subgroups of mothers or infants with distinct risk profiles are rarely explored. This overdependence on classification creates a gap in understanding nuanced relationships between risk factors, disease progression, and outcome severity. As a result, models may achieve good predictive accuracy but fall short in offering comprehensive guidance for healthcare providers.

Blueprint

The primary goal of this project is to develop a Multifactor Health Risk Index for newborns in San Francisco, integrating demographic, medical, and socioeconomic factors to identify infants at high risk for adverse outcomes. The team will begin by preprocessing and cleaning a rich dataset derived from the San Francisco Maternal, Child, and Adolescent Health Needs Assessment, which includes over 83,000 birth records, hospital admissions, emergency room visits, and newborn screenings. Key features such as age, sex, race/ethnicity, insurance type, zip code, and health outcomes will be leveraged to capture a complete picture of each infant‚Äôs risk profile. The first step in analysis will be clustering, which will uncover natural groupings of infants based on multifactorial similarities, revealing hidden patterns in the population. Following this, regression models will quantify the influence of each factor on health outcomes, producing continuous risk scores that reflect the severity or likelihood of complications. These insights will provide both a granular and holistic view of newborn health disparities across different neighborhoods, insurance types, and demographic groups.

Once clusters and regression scores are established, they will be used to define risk categories (low, medium, high) for each newborn, which will serve as class labels for supervised classification models. These models will learn the patterns that determine cluster membership and risk scores, allowing predictions for unseen cases. Our team will explore multiple machine learning approaches including decision trees, random forests, support vector machines, and gradient boosting to optimize predictive performance. To support interpretation and decision-making, the project will include rich visualizations such as heatmaps or cluster diagrams, highlighting disparities and key contributing factors. While the primary dataset is the SF MCAH Needs Assessment, supplemental data may include other health related data. The overarching aim is to create an interpretable machine learning framework that can inform necessary stakeholders in designing targeted interventions for high-risk newborns.

Proposal Overview

The health and well-being of newborns and infants are critical indicators of a community‚Äôs overall public health, yet in many urban areas, including San Francisco, disparities in infant health outcomes persist due to differences in socioeconomic status, access to healthcare, and demographic factors. Some newborns face a higher risk of adverse outcomes such as preterm birth, the need for assisted ventilation, and hospitalization shortly after birth, making it essential to address these disparities to ensure equitable healthcare and reduce preventable complications. Early-life health outcomes not only affect the immediate survival and quality of life of infants but also influence long-term physical and cognitive development. Communities with higher rates of adverse newborn outcomes often experience compounded social and economic challenges, with families facing higher medical costs, stress, and long-term health consequences. By identifying high-risk groups and understanding the factors contributing to poor outcomes, public health interventions can be better targeted to reduce these disparities and save lives. This effort aligns with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goal 3: Good Health and Well-Being, which emphasizes ensuring healthy lives and promoting well-being for all at all ages.

Our proposed solution is to develop a comprehensive health risk assessment framework for newborns using data-driven approaches. By leveraging different social features from city-level public health data, we aim to classify infants into low, medium, and high-risk categories. This will involve clustering to identify natural groupings of newborn health profiles, regression to quantify the influence of different risk factors, and classification to predict risk categories for individual infants. To ensure transparency and interpretability, we will use explainable AI techniques such as SHAP and LIME, along with visualizations that communicate risk patterns clearly to public health stakeholders.

The scope of this project extends beyond mere prediction. It aims to provide actionable insights for healthcare providers, policymakers, and community organizations to identify vulnerable populations, allocate resources effectively, and implement targeted interventions. The framework will also highlight patterns and disparities that may not be immediately visible, helping guide long-term strategies to improve infant health outcomes. Ultimately, this project seeks to combine robust data analysis with clear, interpretable insights that can drive meaningful improvements in public health across the city.

Clustering Analysis

Because the dataset is originally unlabeled, we first apply clustering to group the records into three risk-based clusters: Low Risk, Medium Risk, and High Risk. These cluster assignments are then used as pseudo-labels to train classification models.

K-Means Clustering

K-Means clustering was configured with 3 clusters (n_clusters=3) and a fixed random seed to ensure reproducibility. Cluster centroids were analyzed using the feature Rate_SF_pop.

Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM)

Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) were applied with 3 components (n_components=3) and a full covariance structure. Cluster labels were mapped to risk categories using the mean value of Rate_SF_pop for each component.
